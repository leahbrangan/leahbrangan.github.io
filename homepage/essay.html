The Art of Being Unbothered

I didn’t understand it at first. When I was younger, I used to wonder how people could stay so calm in chaos, how people could just sit in a subway car with two strangers yelling and act like it was nothing.

A few years later, when I was older, I found myself in the same position as many other New Yorkers. I stepped onto the cold, dark 6 train heading back home after school. It was my first time riding the subway by myself. The car rocked forward, metal screeching on the tracks, when a man suddenly started shouting.

I jumped. What’s happening? I looked around and saw a guy shouting at another guy about his bag bumping into him. My heart raced while everyone else sat still. Where I saw danger, they saw another Tuesday night. The woman across from me didn’t even pause her reading. The guy next to her kept watching something on his phone, eyes blank, unbothered.

This was the first time I really saw it — the art of being unbothered.

I’ve ridden the subway since before I could walk or talk, but I never noticed it until then: the way New Yorkers don’t flinch even when the world shakes around them. We learn to navigate chaos. When I walk around now, I don’t even stop when I hear shouting or craziness. I keep moving, headphones in, eyes straight ahead.

Being “unbothered” in New York isn’t indifference. We just let people live their lives with all the chaos that comes with it, without interruption. It’s not about ignoring the world, but choosing to engage with it differently. It may come off as rude to others watching, but beneath that exterior is real kindness and patience.

I learned that again one day when I was rushing somewhere and tripped over a curb. Before I could even stand, the woman sitting on a nearby bench ran over to help me. She asked if I was okay and if I needed anything. She didn’t know me, and I didn’t catch her name, but she still cared.

That’s the paradox of New York: people seem distant, but they’re always watching, always ready to help when it matters.

Everyone here seems to be in their own world, chasing their own goals, but that’s not the full truth. We’re all in the same boat. We ride the same trains, walk the same cracked sidewalks, and stand in the same long lines. We’ve all felt the sting of a delayed train, the rush of catching it just in time, the quiet relief of finding a seat after a long day.

The city tests us and rewards us in equal measure. It humbles us, lifts us, wears us down, and builds us back up. We share everything: the frustration, the laughter, the small miracles hidden in ordinary days.

Sometimes when I walk home late at night, I see faces lit by streetlights, blank at first but full of quiet stories. Beneath the tired eyes and hurried steps, there’s a softness — a shared understanding that we’re all just trying to make it.

We act tough because the city demands it, but underneath it all, New Yorkers carry an unspoken empathy, a silent kind of care that only those who live here can recognize.

After spending my whole life here, I started to notice the rhythm of it all — the rush, the stillness, the chaos, the calm. Even when everything feels loud, there’s a strange harmony underneath.

Now when I think back to that night on the 6 train, I see it differently. The silence wasn’t ignoring; it was understanding. Everyone there knew the weight of the day, the noise they had already endured, the patience it takes just to exist here.

I’ve come to believe that being unbothered is its own kind of love — for yourself, for the city, for the chaos that refuses to quiet down. It’s learning when to pause and when to move, when to watch and when to act.

Sometimes I find myself on that same 6 train heading downtown, older and calmer now. The doors close. The train rumbles. The city hums around me. Someone is shouting again somewhere down the car.

But I just smile. I stay still. I look out the window at the blur of tunnels rushing past.

Maybe we are rude. Maybe we don’t care. But sitting there, surrounded by all that noise and motion, I think we do — just in our own quiet way.
